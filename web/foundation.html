Foundation

Who stewards Loon, why it is structured this way, and the values behind every decision.

The Steward

Loon is stewarded by MPLS LLC, founded by Patrick in East Bethel, Minnesota.

The model is deliberate: a company holds stewardship of the compiler and toolchain. The .org domain — loonlang.org — projects community ownership. The language belongs to the people who use it. The steward maintains it, funds development, and makes decisions until governance structures exist to share that responsibility.

This is the same model Rust used with Mozilla. A single organization provides continuity, legal standing, and the ability to act quickly. The community provides direction, contribution, and accountability. Neither works without the other.

Today the steward is one person. That is honest, not aspirational. The project is pre-1.0. The community is small. Solo stewardship at this stage means decisions happen fast, the vision stays coherent, and no one pretends there is a committee where there is not one.

The Lineage

Loon stands in a tradition that spans forty years:

  • GNU (1983) — Software freedom. Richard Stallman wrote the GPL manifesto before GNU was finished. The values came first. The software grew to fill them.
  • GIMP (1995) — Creative tools. The GNU Image Manipulation Program proved that free software could compete with commercial tools in domains that matter to individual creators.
  • MPLS (2025) — The agentic era. AI writes most of the code. The compiler must catch what humans forget to specify and what AI does not know to check.

Each one a tool built on values, named with intention, owned by the community that uses it. Same spirit. New threats. New instrument.

The recursive name is part of the tradition. GNU stands for "GNU's Not Unix." MPLS stands for "MPLS Principled Libre Software." The recursion is a signal: this project knows where it comes from and does not pretend to be the first of its kind.

Why MPLS

MPLS — MPLS Principled Libre Software. A recursive acronym in the GNU tradition. From Minneapolis. Values that do not bend.

The name encodes three commitments:

  • Principled. The prohibited uses in the license are not legal boilerplate. They are values. Surveillance, weapons, oppression, manipulation — no license tier, no payment, no exception unlocks them. The git history shows these commitments were made before the language was useful to anyone. That sequence is the proof of intent.
  • Libre. Free as in freedom, not just free as in price. The source is in the repository. The bootstrap process is documented and reproducible. The compiler has no vendored blobs, no opaque dependencies, no trust assumptions you cannot verify.
  • Software. This is a tool, not a movement. It compiles programs. It enforces privacy types. It catches credential leaks at compile time. The values exist to serve the software, and the software exists to serve the people who use it.

What We Believe

People first. Always. When the interests of institutions conflict with the interests of people, people win. This is not balanced against business needs. It is the tiebreaker.

Creators own what they build. If you conceived it, directed it, and shaped it — you made it. What you made is yours. This includes work created with AI assistance. The idea was yours. The direction was yours. The judgment of what to keep and what to discard was yours. That is authorship. The tool does not own the work. The platform does not own the work. You do.

Safety is the default, not the option. Privacy from governments. Privacy from corporations. Privacy from bad actors. Protection from systems built to exploit. These are not features to be enabled. They are the ground state. In the language, this manifests as privacy types that the compiler enforces without anyone asking it to. Sensitive<String> cannot be logged. That is not a setting. It is the type system.

Freedom and protection belong together. The free software movement gave us the right to see the code. The MPLS License gives us the right to control what it does and who benefits from it. You can have both. The creativity to build anything. The openness for everyone to benefit. The protection this moment requires.

Governance

Loon has a solo steward today. Patrick makes the design decisions, reviews contributions, and maintains the compiler. This is appropriate for a pre-1.0 project with a small community.

As adoption grows, governance will evolve:

  • Year 1 — Solo stewardship with public decision-making. Design rationale documented in the repository. Community feedback welcomed through GitHub issues and discussions.
  • Year 2–3 — Advisory structure. Contributors who have demonstrated sustained engagement and alignment with the project's values participate in design decisions. No formal voting yet — consensus among trusted contributors.
  • Year 3+ — Foundation structure to be evaluated. If Loon reaches the scale where a single steward is a bottleneck or a risk, the project will transition to a foundation model. The .org domain, the specification, and the test suite transfer to the foundation. MPLS LLC retains commercial licensing rights to fund ongoing development.

This timeline is not a promise. It is a direction. The specifics will depend on who shows up, what they need, and whether the project reaches a scale where shared governance is necessary rather than performative.

The License

Loon is released under the MPLS Principled Libre Software License v1.0.

The short version: free for individuals, small organizations (under $1M revenue and under 100K users), nonprofits, educators, and open source projects. Large organizations contribute back or obtain a commercial license.

Certain uses are prohibited unconditionally: surveillance, weapons, oppression, manipulation, AI training extraction without permission, and secret government compliance orders. No license tier unlocks them. No payment unlocks them. The values are not for sale.

Programs you compile with Loon belong to you. The license applies to the compiler and toolchain, not to the output. AI-assisted work belongs to the human who directed it.

The full license text, values, and reasoning are in the repository. Read the license page or the PHILOSOPHY.md for the complete record.
